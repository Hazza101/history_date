AD America, 1920-1973: Opportunity and Inequality

Exam Board: AQA History

Paper: Paper 1 (Understanding the Modern World)

Section: Section A (Period Studies)

The ratification of the 19th Amendment granted women the right to vote, a significant milestone in the struggle for gender equality and political participation in the United States.

The Wall Street Crash of 1929, also known as Black Tuesday, marked the beginning of the Great Depression, causing widespread economic hardship and unemployment across the United States.

President Franklin D. Roosevelt launched the New Deal programs to address the economic challenges of the Great Depression, implementing reforms and relief measures to stimulate recovery and alleviate poverty.

The Social Security Act established a system of social insurance, providing financial assistance to retirees, the unemployed, and people with disabilities, laying the foundation for America's modern welfare state.

The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor brought the United States into World War II, leading to its full-scale involvement in the conflict and shaping its role as a global superpower.

The end of World War II marked a period of economic prosperity and global leadership for the United States, with the country emerging as a dominant force in international affairs.

The Supreme Court's ruling in Brown v. Board of Education declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional, a landmark decision in the Civil Rights Movement that paved the way for desegregation and equal educational opportunities.

President Lyndon B. Johnson signed the Civil Rights Act into law, prohibiting discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin, and enforcing voting rights and desegregation.

The assassination of civil rights leader Martin Luther King Jr. sparked nationwide outrage and mourning, underscoring the ongoing struggle for racial equality and social justice in America.

The end of the Vietnam War marked the conclusion of America's involvement in the conflict, leading to profound social and political divisions and raising questions about U.S. foreign policy and military intervention.
