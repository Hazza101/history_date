The Cold War in Europe 1945-1961: Rising Tensions and The changing international order after 1945 and its consequences

Exam Board: OCR History A (Explaining the Modern World)

Paper: Component Group 1

Section: International Relations: The Changing International Order 1918-1975

The Yalta Conference was a meeting between the leaders of the Allied powers—Winston Churchill, Franklin D. Roosevelt, and Joseph Stalin—held in Yalta, Crimea, to discuss the post-war reorganization of Europe and the establishment of peace. The conference resulted in agreements on the division of Germany, the formation of the United Nations, and Soviet entry into the war against Japan.

The Truman Doctrine was a policy announced by President Harry Truman, pledging to support countries threatened by communist expansionism. It represented a shift in U.S. foreign policy towards containment of Soviet influence and marked the beginning of the Cold War rivalry between the United States and the Soviet Union.

The Marshall Plan, officially known as the European Recovery Program, was an American initiative to provide economic aid to Western Europe in the aftermath of World War II. The plan aimed to rebuild war-torn economies, foster economic stability, and prevent the spread of communism, contributing to the post-war recovery and prosperity of Europe.

The Berlin Airlift was a massive humanitarian operation undertaken by the United States, United Kingdom, and other Western allies to supply West Berlin with food, fuel, and other essentials during the Soviet blockade of the city. The airlift demonstrated Western resolve and solidarity in the face of Soviet aggression and contributed to the stabilization of post-war Europe.

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) was established as a collective defense alliance between North American and European countries, aimed at countering the threat of Soviet expansionism in Europe. NATO's formation represented a cornerstone of Western security policy during the Cold War and contributed to the containment of Soviet influence.

The Warsaw Pact was a military alliance formed by the Soviet Union and its Eastern European satellite states in response to the establishment of NATO. The pact aimed to counterbalance Western military power and solidify Soviet control over Eastern Europe, marking a significant escalation in Cold War tensions and the division of Europe.

The Geneva Summit was a meeting between the leaders of the United States, Soviet Union, United Kingdom, and France, held in Geneva, Switzerland, to discuss Cold War tensions and arms control. Although no major agreements were reached, the summit helped to ease tensions and set the stage for future diplomatic initiatives.

The Hungarian Revolution was a nationwide uprising against Soviet-imposed policies and the Hungarian Communist government, led by reformist elements seeking political and economic freedom. The revolution was brutally suppressed by Soviet forces, highlighting the challenges of asserting independence in Soviet-dominated Eastern Europe.

The launch of Sputnik, the world's first artificial satellite, by the Soviet Union marked the beginning of the space age and intensified the space race between the United States and the Soviet Union. Sputnik's successful orbiting had profound implications for science, technology, and the Cold War rivalry between the superpowers.

The construction of the Berlin Wall by the East German government, with Soviet support, sealed off West Berlin from East Berlin and surrounding areas, dividing the city and symbolizing the broader division between East and West during the Cold War. The wall became a potent symbol of communist oppression and the Iron Curtain separating Eastern and Western Europe.
