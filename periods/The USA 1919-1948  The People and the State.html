The USA 1919-1948: The People and the State

Exam Board: OCR History A (Explaining the Modern World)

Paper: Component Group 1

Section: Non-British Depth Study

The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor, Hawaii, on December 7, 1941, prompted the United States to enter World War II. The surprise attack resulted in significant casualties and damage to the U.S. Pacific Fleet and led to the declaration of war against Japan the following day, marking the beginning of America's involvement in the global conflict.

President Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066 on February 19, 1942, authorizing the forced relocation and internment of Japanese Americans living on the West Coast of the United States. The order, motivated by fears of espionage and sabotage following the Pearl Harbor attack, led to the displacement and confinement of over 120,000 Japanese Americans, many of whom were American citizens.

The Zoot Suit Riots were a series of racially charged conflicts that occurred in Los Angeles, California, in 1943. The riots pitted American servicemen, predominantly white, against Mexican American youths who wore zoot suits, a style associated with urban African American and Latino communities. The riots reflected tensions over race, ethnicity, and cultural identity during World War II.

The Servicemen's Readjustment Act of 1944, commonly known as the G.I. Bill of Rights, was signed into law on June 22, 1944. The bill provided a range of benefits to World War II veterans, including access to education, vocational training, home loans, unemployment compensation, and healthcare, facilitating their reintegration into civilian life and contributing to post-war economic prosperity and social mobility.

World War II officially ended with the unconditional surrender of Japan on September 2, 1945, following the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki and the Soviet invasion of Manchuria. The conclusion of the war brought about a profound sense of relief and jubilation among Americans, marking the beginning of a new era of peace and reconstruction.

The Manhattan Project was a top-secret research and development program undertaken by the United States during World War II to develop the first atomic bombs. Led by scientists such as J. Robert Oppenheimer and Enrico Fermi, the project culminated in the successful testing of the atomic bomb.

President Harry S. Truman issued Executive Order 9981 on July 26, 1948, ordering the integration of the U.S. Armed Forces and the elimination of segregation and discrimination based on race, color, religion, or national origin. The order represented a significant milestone in the civil rights movement and paved the way for desegregation in other areas of American society.

The Women's Armed Services Integration Act, enacted on June 12, 1948, allowed women to serve as full members of the United States Armed Forces for the first time in American history. The act recognized the valuable contributions of women to the military effort during World War II and expanded opportunities for women in the armed services.

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) was established on April 4, 1949, as a collective defense alliance between North American and European countries. NATO's formation was prompted by concerns over Soviet expansionism and aggression in the aftermath of World War II and represented a commitment to mutual defense and security among member states.

The landmark Supreme Court case Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka, decided on May 17, 1954, declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional, overturning the "separate but equal" doctrine established by the Plessy v. Ferguson decision in 1896. The ruling marked a significant victory for the civil rights movement and paved the way for desegregation in public education and other areas of American life.
