South Africa 1960-1994: The People and the State

Exam Board: OCR History A (Explaining the Modern World)

Paper: Component Group 1

Section: Non-British Depth Study

The 1980s witnessed a surge in anti-apartheid protests across South Africa, as well as internationally. Activists, including trade unions, students, and religious groups, organized demonstrations, strikes, and boycotts to demand an end to apartheid and the release of political prisoners. The protests played a crucial role in mobilizing opposition to the apartheid regime.

The 1984-1985 Miners' Strike, also known as the Rand Rebellion, was a mass strike by black miners in South Africa's mining industry. The strike, sparked by grievances over low wages and poor working conditions, paralyzed the country's economy and drew international attention to the plight of black workers under apartheid.

Nelson Mandela, the iconic leader of the anti-apartheid movement and the African National Congress (ANC), was released from prison on February 11, 1990, after serving 27 years behind bars. Mandela's release marked a significant turning point in South Africa's history and paved the way for negotiations to end apartheid.

In a landmark decision, South African President F.W. de Klerk announced the legalization of the African National Congress (ANC), the Pan Africanist Congress (PAC), and other anti-apartheid organizations on February 2, 1990. The legalization allowed these organizations to participate openly in the country's political process.

The Groote Schuur Minute, signed on May 4, 1990, by President F.W. de Klerk and Nelson Mandela, marked the beginning of formal negotiations between the apartheid government and the ANC. The minute outlined the principles and framework for negotiations aimed at ending apartheid and establishing a democratic South Africa.

Multiparty negotiations, known as the Convention for a Democratic South Africa (CODESA), took place between 1991 and 1993, involving representatives from the apartheid government, the ANC, and other political parties. The negotiations focused on key issues such as constitutional reform, political representation, and the transition to majority rule.

The Interim Constitution of South Africa was adopted on November 18, 1993, as part of the negotiated transition from apartheid to democracy. The constitution established a transitional government of national unity and laid the groundwork for the country's first democratic elections in 1994.

South Africa held its first democratic elections on April 27, 1994, in which all races were allowed to vote for the first time. The elections, won by the African National Congress (ANC) led by Nelson Mandela, marked the end of apartheid and the beginning of a new era of democracy and reconciliation in South Africa.

Nelson Mandela was inaugurated as South Africa's first black president on May 10, 1994, following the historic democratic elections earlier that year. Mandela's inauguration symbolized the triumph of democracy over apartheid and the beginning of a new era of hope and reconciliation for the country.

South Africa's final constitution, the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, was adopted on December 4, 1996. The constitution enshrined the principles of democracy, human rights, and equality, and established the framework for a nonracial, nonsexist, and democratic society in South Africa.
