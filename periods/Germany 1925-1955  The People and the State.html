Germany 1925-1955: The People and the State

Exam Board: OCR History A (Explaining the Modern World)

Paper: Component Group 1

Section: Non-British Depth Study

The invasion of Poland by Nazi Germany on September 1, 1939, marked the beginning of World War II. The invasion, carried out with Soviet support, led to the rapid defeat of Poland and the subsequent occupation of the country by German forces.

The Battle of Stalingrad, fought between German and Soviet forces from August 23, 1942, to February 2, 1943, was one of the largest and bloodiest battles in history. The Soviet victory at Stalingrad marked a turning point in the war, halting the German advance into the Soviet Union and paving the way for Allied victory in Europe.

The D-Day landings, which took place on June 6, 1944, were the largest amphibious invasion in history and marked the beginning of the Allied liberation of German-occupied Western Europe during World War II. The successful landings allowed Allied forces to establish a foothold in Normandy and eventually push German forces back towards Germany.

The atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki by the United States on August 6 and 9, 1945, respectively, were the first and only use of nuclear weapons in warfare. The bombings resulted in the deaths of over 100,000 people and the eventual surrender of Japan, leading to the end of World War II.

The Nuremberg Trials, which began on November 20, 1945, were a series of military tribunals held to prosecute prominent leaders of Nazi Germany for war crimes, crimes against humanity, and other offenses committed during World War II. The trials established the principle that individuals could be held accountable for actions committed during wartime, regardless of their official position.

The United Nations (UN) was established on October 24, 1945, in the aftermath of World War II to promote international cooperation and prevent future conflicts. The UN replaced the ineffective League of Nations and has since played a central role in global diplomacy, peacekeeping, and humanitarian efforts.

The Berlin Blockade, imposed by the Soviet Union on June 24, 1948, was an attempt to cut off Western access to West Berlin. In response, the United States and its allies organized the Berlin Airlift, a massive humanitarian operation to supply West Berlin with food, fuel, and other essentials, which lasted until May 12, 1949, and successfully thwarted the blockade.

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) was established as a collective defense alliance between North American and European countries on April 4, 1949. NATO's formation represented a response to the perceived threat of Soviet expansionism and played a central role in maintaining stability and security in Europe during the Cold War.

The People's Republic of China was proclaimed by Mao Zedong on October 1, 1949, following the victory of the Chinese Communist Party in the Chinese Civil War. The founding of the People's Republic marked the culmination of decades of struggle and marked the beginning of Communist rule in China.

The Geneva Accords, signed on July 21, 1954, ended the First Indochina War and temporarily divided Vietnam into two separate states: North Vietnam and South Vietnam. The accords also called for free elections to reunify Vietnam, which ultimately never took place, leading to further conflict in the region.
