Cold War confrontations and conflict 1954–1975 (Developing Cold War tensions and conflicts)

Exam Board: OCR History A (Explaining the Modern World)

Paper: Component Group 1

Section: International Relations: The Changing International Order 1918-1975

The Geneva Accords were a set of agreements reached at the Geneva Conference, which aimed to resolve the First Indochina War and establish peace in Vietnam. The accords led to the division of Vietnam into North and South, with elections planned for reunification. However, the elections were never held, setting the stage for further conflict.

In response to the Hungarian Revolution, Soviet forces, along with other Warsaw Pact countries, invaded Hungary to crush the uprising and maintain communist control. The invasion marked a significant escalation in Cold War tensions and highlighted the Soviet Union's determination to suppress dissent in its satellite states.

The launch of Sputnik, the first artificial satellite, by the Soviet Union ignited the Space Race between the United States and the Soviet Union. The competition for space exploration supremacy became a key aspect of the Cold War rivalry, with both superpowers striving for technological and ideological dominance.

The Cuban Missile Crisis was a 13-day confrontation between the United States and the Soviet Union over Soviet ballistic missiles deployed in Cuba. The crisis brought the world to the brink of nuclear war and is regarded as one of the tensest moments of the Cold War. It ended with a diplomatic resolution and the removal of missiles from Cuba.

The Vietnam War was a prolonged conflict between the communist government of North Vietnam, supported by the Soviet Union and China, and the non-communist government of South Vietnam, supported by the United States and other anti-communist allies. The war was a major battleground of the Cold War and had significant geopolitical implications.

The Bay of Pigs Invasion was a failed CIA-led operation to overthrow the Cuban government of Fidel Castro by landing Cuban exiles on the southern coast of Cuba. The invasion was a humiliation for the United States and intensified Cold War tensions between the U.S. and the Soviet Union, which supported Cuba.

The Prague Spring was a period of political liberalization and reform in Czechoslovakia under the leadership of Alexander Dubček. The reforms aimed to create "socialism with a human face" but were met with opposition from the Soviet Union, which led to the invasion of Czechoslovakia by Warsaw Pact forces to suppress the movement.

The Six-Day War was a brief but significant conflict between Israel and its Arab neighbors—Egypt, Jordan, and Syria. Israel's victory in the war resulted in the capture of the Sinai Peninsula, Gaza Strip, West Bank, and Golan Heights, dramatically altering the geopolitics of the Middle East and exacerbating Cold War tensions.

The Paris Peace Accords were a set of agreements signed to establish peace in Vietnam and end the Vietnam War. The accords led to a ceasefire, the withdrawal of U.S. troops from Vietnam, and the release of prisoners of war. However, the agreements ultimately failed to bring lasting peace to the region.

The fall of Saigon, the capital of South Vietnam, to North Vietnamese forces marked the end of the Vietnam War and the reunification of Vietnam under communist rule. The war's conclusion was a significant setback for the United States and represented a victory for communist forces, reshaping the geopolitical landscape of Southeast Asia.
