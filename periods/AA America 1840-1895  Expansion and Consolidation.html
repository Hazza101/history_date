AA America 1840-1895: Expansion and Consolidation

Exam Board: AQA History

Paper: Paper 1 (Understanding the Modern World)

Section: Section A (Period Studies)

The Annexation of Texas occurred when Texas was admitted to the United States as the 28th state, sparking tensions with Mexico and eventually leading to the Mexican-American War.

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo ended the Mexican-American War, resulting in Mexico ceding significant territory to the United States, including California, Nevada, and parts of Arizona, Colorado, Utah, and New Mexico.

The Gadsden Purchase involved the United States acquiring a strip of land from Mexico for the purpose of building a southern transcontinental railroad, ultimately shaping the modern-day borders of Arizona and New Mexico.

The Civil War began with the Confederate attack on Fort Sumter, marking a pivotal moment in American history characterized by conflict over issues of slavery and states' rights.

The Homestead Act was signed into law, encouraging Western migration by granting settlers 160 acres of public land in exchange for a commitment to live on and cultivate the land.

The United States purchased Alaska from Russia for $7.2 million, expanding its territory into the Pacific Northwest and securing valuable resources.

The completion of the first transcontinental railroad connected the eastern and western United States, facilitating transportation and communication across the country and promoting economic growth.

Also known as Custer's Last Stand, the Battle of Little Bighorn was a significant defeat for U.S. forces against Lakota, Northern Cheyenne, and Arapaho tribes, highlighting ongoing conflicts over land and resources in the West.

The Dawes Act aimed to assimilate Native Americans by granting them individual land allotments and opening up remaining tribal lands for white settlement.

The Wounded Knee Massacre marked the end of major Indian resistance to white expansion in the West and represented the culmination of decades of conflict between Native Americans and the U.S. government over land and cultural autonomy.
