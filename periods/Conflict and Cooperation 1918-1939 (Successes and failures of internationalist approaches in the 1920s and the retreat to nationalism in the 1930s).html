Conflict and Cooperation 1918-1939 (Successes and failures of internationalist approaches in the 1920s and the retreat to nationalism in the 1930s)

Exam Board: OCR History A (Explaining the Modern World)

Paper: Component Group 1

Section: International Relations: The Changing International Order 1918-1975

The League of Nations was established as the first worldwide intergovernmental organization aimed at promoting peace and cooperation among nations after World War I. It was founded on the principles of collective security and the resolution of disputes through diplomacy and negotiation.

The Treaty of Versailles was one of the peace treaties that ended World War I, negotiated at the Paris Peace Conference. It imposed heavy reparations and territorial losses on Germany, contributing to economic instability and resentment that would later fuel the rise of nationalism and militarism in Germany.

The Washington Naval Conference was a multilateral conference held in Washington, D.C., aimed at addressing naval disarmament and maintaining peace in the aftermath of World War I. The conference resulted in several treaties that limited naval armaments and established ratios for capital ships among the major powers.

The Locarno Treaties were a series of agreements signed in Locarno, Switzerland, aimed at ensuring peace and stability in Europe after World War I. The treaties, which included mutual guarantees of borders and arbitration of disputes, marked a period of d√©tente and cooperation among European powers.

The General Strike of 1926 was a nationwide strike in Britain involving millions of workers across various industries, organized in support of coal miners who were facing wage cuts and worsening working conditions. The strike, which lasted nine days, highlighted social and economic tensions in interwar Britain and the challenges of industrial unrest.

The Statute of Westminster was an act of the British Parliament that granted legislative independence to the dominions of the British Empire, including Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and South Africa. It marked a significant step towards the decolonization of the British Empire and the recognition of self-governing dominions as equal partners.

The Geneva Disarmament Conference was an international conference convened to address disarmament and prevent the arms race that followed World War I. Despite initial optimism, the conference failed to reach significant agreements due to deep-seated distrust and competing national interests among the major powers.

King Edward VIII abdicated the British throne in a constitutional crisis precipitated by his desire to marry Wallis Simpson, an American divorcee. His abdication raised questions about the role of the monarchy and highlighted tensions between personal desires and public duty in interwar Britain.

The Munich Agreement was a diplomatic settlement reached between Germany, Great Britain, France, and Italy, allowing Nazi Germany to annex the Sudetenland, a region of Czechoslovakia inhabited by ethnic Germans. The agreement, seen as a policy of appeasement, failed to prevent further aggression by Nazi Germany and contributed to the outbreak of World War II.

World War II began with the German invasion of Poland, triggering a cascade of events that led to the declaration of war by Britain and France against Germany. The outbreak of war marked the failure of international efforts to maintain peace and resolve conflicts through diplomacy, culminating in a global conflagration that would reshape the course of history.
