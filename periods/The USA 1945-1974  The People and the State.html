The landmark Supreme Court case Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka, decided on May 17, 1954, declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional, overturning the "separate but equal" doctrine established by the Plessy v. Ferguson decision in 1896. The ruling marked a significant victory for the civil rights movement and paved the way for desegregation in public education and other areas of American life.

The Montgomery Bus Boycott was a seminal event in the civil rights movement that began on December 1, 1955, when Rosa Parks, an African American woman, refused to give up her seat to a white passenger on a segregated bus in Montgomery, Alabama. The boycott, led by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. and the Montgomery Improvement Association, lasted for 381 days and ultimately resulted in the desegregation of the city's public transportation system.

The Little Rock Nine were a group of African American students who were enrolled in Little Rock Central High School in Arkansas in 1957 following the Supreme Court's ruling in Brown v. Board of Education. Their enrollment was met with violent opposition from white segregationists and required the intervention of federal troops ordered by President Dwight D. Eisenhower to ensure their safety and enforce desegregation.

The Civil Rights Act of 1957 was the first federal civil rights legislation passed since Reconstruction. Signed into law by President Dwight D. Eisenhower on September 9, 1957, the act aimed to protect the voting rights of African Americans by establishing a federal Civil Rights Commission to investigate voting discrimination and enforce voting rights laws.

The Greensboro Sit-Ins began on February 1, 1960, when four African American college students from North Carolina Agricultural and Technical State University sat at a segregated lunch counter at Woolworth's in Greensboro, North Carolina, and demanded service. Their peaceful protest sparked a wave of sit-ins and demonstrations across the South, leading to the desegregation of many public accommodations.

The Freedom Rides were a series of bus trips undertaken by civil rights activists in 1961 to challenge segregation on interstate buses and bus terminals in the South. Organized by the Congress of Racial Equality (CORE), the rides were met with violent opposition from white supremacists, leading to clashes and arrests. The Freedom Rides drew national attention to the continued resistance to desegregation and the need for federal intervention to enforce civil rights laws.

The Birmingham Campaign was a coordinated series of nonviolent protests and civil disobedience actions led by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. and the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC) in Birmingham, Alabama, in 1963. The campaign aimed to challenge segregation and racial injustice in one of the most racially divided cities in the South and drew widespread attention to the brutality of segregationist tactics, including the use of police dogs and fire hoses against peaceful demonstrators.

The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom took place on August 28, 1963, in Washington, D.C., and was attended by an estimated 250,000 people, making it one of the largest demonstrations for civil rights in American history. The highlight of the march was Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.'s iconic "I Have a Dream" speech, which called for an end to racism and envisioned a future of equality and justice for all Americans.

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was landmark legislation signed into law by President Lyndon B. Johnson on July 2, 1964. The act outlawed discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin and prohibited segregation in public accommodations, employment, and federally funded programs. It represented a major victory for the civil rights movement and marked a significant step toward achieving racial equality in the United States.
