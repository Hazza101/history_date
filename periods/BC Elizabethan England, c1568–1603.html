The Act of Supremacy was passed by Parliament, declaring Queen Elizabeth I as the Supreme Governor of the Church of England. This legislation affirmed royal authority over religious matters and marked the official establishment of Protestantism in England.

Queen Elizabeth I presided over the foundation of the Church of England as the state church, asserting royal supremacy over religious matters. The Elizabethan Religious Settlement established a moderate Protestant religious establishment, seeking to strike a balance between Catholic and Puritan factions in England.

The Royal Exchange, a center for commerce and trade, was established by Queen Elizabeth I in London. It served as a marketplace for merchants and traders to conduct business transactions and played a significant role in the economic prosperity of Elizabethan England.

Mary, Queen of Scots, was executed at Fotheringhay Castle in Northamptonshire on the orders of Queen Elizabeth I of England, following her implication in various plots against Elizabeth's throne, including the Babington Plot. This event marked a decisive moment in the Tudor succession crisis and Anglo-Scottish relations.

The Spanish Armada, a fleet sent by King Philip II of Spain to invade England and overthrow Queen Elizabeth I, was defeated by the English navy under the command of Sir Francis Drake and Lord Howard. The decisive naval battle, which took place in the English Channel, secured England's maritime supremacy and enhanced national pride.

The Elizabethan Poor Law was enacted by Parliament, formalizing the system of poor relief and welfare provision in England. The law established a framework for local parishes to provide assistance to the needy, including the construction of workhouses and the imposition of local taxes to support the poor. This legislation reflected concerns about poverty, vagrancy, and social disorder during the Elizabethan era.

Robert Devereux, the 2nd Earl of Essex and a former favorite of Queen Elizabeth I, was executed for his role in a failed rebellion against the Queen's government. His execution marked the downfall of a prominent Elizabethan nobleman and highlighted the challenges of maintaining royal authority during the later years of Elizabeth's reign.

The East India Company, a joint-stock company established by royal charter, was founded to facilitate trade with the East Indies. This commercial venture played a significant role in English overseas expansion and the development of England's maritime trade networks during the Elizabethan period.

Queen Elizabeth I passed away at Richmond Palace, marking the end of her reign and the Elizabethan era. Her death paved the way for the accession of King James VI of Scotland as King James I of England, heralding the beginning of the Stuart period in English history.
