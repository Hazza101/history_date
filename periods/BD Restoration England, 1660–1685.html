The monarchy was restored in England with the return of King Charles II to the throne after the period of republican rule under Oliver Cromwell. This marked the end of the Interregnum and the beginning of the Restoration era, characterized by the reestablishment of the monarchy, the Church of England, and the aristocratic order.

The Declaration of Breda, issued by King Charles II, outlined his terms for the restoration of the monarchy and the settlement of political and religious issues in England. The declaration offered amnesty to former enemies and promised religious toleration, paving the way for a peaceful transition of power and the restoration of stability.

The Great Plague of London was a devastating outbreak of bubonic plague that swept through the city, resulting in tens of thousands of deaths and widespread panic. The epidemic, which reached its peak in 1665, had profound social, economic, and psychological impacts on Restoration England.

The Great Fire of London, one of the most significant disasters in English history, destroyed much of the city, including thousands of homes, churches, and public buildings. The fire, which raged for three days, led to extensive rebuilding efforts and the implementation of new fire safety regulations.

The Navigation Acts were a series of laws enacted by the English Parliament to regulate colonial trade and navigation and to protect English shipping and commerce. These acts, which imposed restrictions on colonial trade and favored English merchants, played a key role in shaping the economic policies of Restoration England.

The Treaty of Dover was a secret treaty between England and France, negotiated by King Charles II and King Louis XIV, which aimed to strengthen the Anglo-French alliance against the Dutch Republic and Spain. The treaty included provisions for military cooperation and mutual support, as well as a secret clause committing England to Catholic conversion.

The Popish Plot was a fictitious conspiracy fabricated by Titus Oates, a discredited clergyman, alleging a Catholic plot to assassinate King Charles II and overthrow the Protestant government. The plot, which led to the persecution of Catholics and the execution of innocent individuals, fueled anti-Catholic hysteria and political instability in Restoration England.

The Exclusion Crisis was a political crisis in England triggered by attempts to exclude King Charles II's Catholic brother, James, Duke of York, from the line of succession. The crisis, fueled by fears of Catholic succession and absolutist tendencies, led to intense political polarization and debates over royal authority and religious toleration.

The Glorious Revolution was a bloodless revolution that resulted in the overthrow of King James II and the accession of William III and Mary II to the English throne. The revolution, sparked by fears of Catholic absolutism and James's disregard for parliamentary sovereignty, established constitutional monarchy and laid the foundations for modern parliamentary democracy in England.

The Bill of Rights was a landmark constitutional document enacted by the English Parliament, affirming the rights and liberties of the English people and limiting the powers of the monarchy. The bill, which established principles of constitutional monarchy, rule of law, and parliamentary supremacy, played a crucial role in shaping the political and legal framework of Restoration England.
