AB Germany, 1890-1945: Democracy and Dictatorship

Exam Board: AQA History

Paper: Paper 1 (Understanding the Modern World)

Section: Section A (Period Studies)

Kaiser Wilhelm II dismissed Chancellor Otto von Bismarck, signaling a shift in German domestic and foreign policies and contributing to the decline of Bismarck's conservative influence.

The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria-Hungary in Sarajevo triggered the outbreak of World War I, with Germany's support for Austria-Hungary leading to a wider conflict.

The German Revolution resulted in the abdication of Kaiser Wilhelm II and the establishment of the Weimar Republic, marking the end of the German monarchy and the beginning of a tumultuous period of political change.

Adolf Hitler was appointed Chancellor of Germany, marking the beginning of the Nazi regime and the erosion of democratic institutions as Hitler consolidated power.

The Reichstag fire provided the pretext for the Nazi regime to suppress political opposition, leading to the suspension of civil liberties and the consolidation of Nazi control over the German state.

The Night of the Long Knives saw Hitler's purge of the SA leadership and other political rivals, solidifying his authority within the Nazi Party and eliminating potential threats to his leadership.

Kristallnacht, or the Night of Broken Glass, marked a violent escalation of anti-Semitic persecution in Germany, with widespread attacks on Jewish homes, businesses, and synagogues by Nazi forces and civilians.

The Invasion of Poland by Germany marked the beginning of World War II in Europe, as Hitler's aggressive expansionist policies threatened peace and stability on the continent.

The Battle of Stalingrad was a turning point in World War II, with the Soviet victory over German forces marking the beginning of the end for Nazi Germany's military ambitions in the East.

The end of World War II in Europe came with Germany's unconditional surrender, leading to the collapse of the Nazi regime and the division of Germany into East and West under Allied occupation.
