AA Britain: Health and the people: c1000 to the present day

Exam Board: AQA History

Paper: Paper 2 (Shaping the Nation)

Section: Section A (Thematic Studies)

The Black Death, a bubonic plague pandemic, ravaged Britain, causing widespread death and social upheaval, leading to labor shortages, economic disruptions, and changes in medieval society.

The National Health Service (NHS) was established in Britain, providing universal healthcare free at the point of use, representing a landmark in public health policy and improving access to medical services for all citizens.

The Great Plague of London struck the city, resulting in significant loss of life and forcing authorities to implement measures such as quarantine and sanitation improvements to control the spread of disease.

The Industrial Revolution led to urbanization and poor living conditions in Britain's cities, prompting public health reforms such as sanitation improvements, clean water initiatives, and the establishment of boards of health to address disease outbreaks.

Scottish scientist Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin, the first antibiotic drug, revolutionizing medicine and leading to the development of new treatments for bacterial infections, saving countless lives and improving public health.

Cholera epidemics swept through Britain during the 19th century, particularly affecting densely populated urban areas, prompting investigations into the causes of the disease and improvements in sanitation and hygiene practices.

The Clean Air Act was passed in response to London's Great Smog of 1952, which caused thousands of deaths from respiratory illnesses, leading to regulations to reduce air pollution and protect public health.

The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) was established to provide evidence-based guidance and recommendations for healthcare and social care in England, aiming to improve the quality and efficiency of health services.

The NHS vaccination program was introduced in Britain, offering free vaccinations to protect against infectious diseases such as measles, polio, and tuberculosis, contributing to the decline of these diseases and promoting public health.

Surgeon Sir Terence English performed the first successful human-to-human heart transplant in Britain at the National Heart Hospital in London, pioneering organ transplantation and advancing medical treatments for cardiovascular diseases.
