AB Britain: Power and the people: c1170 to the present day

Exam Board: AQA History

Paper: Paper 2 (Shaping the Nation)

Section: Section A (Thematic Studies)

King John of England sealed the Magna Carta, a document that limited the powers of the monarchy and established principles of justice and the rule of law, laying the foundation for constitutional government in Britain.

The English Civil War erupted between supporters of King Charles I (Royalists) and Parliamentarians, leading to a series of armed conflicts over issues of governance, religion, and individual liberties, ultimately resulting in the trial and execution of Charles I and the establishment of a republic.

The Reform Act of 1832 was passed in Britain, expanding voting rights and reforming electoral constituencies, representing a significant step towards representative democracy and the inclusion of more people in the political process.

The Chartist movement emerged in Britain, advocating for political reforms such as universal male suffrage, secret ballots, and annual parliamentary elections, reflecting growing demands for democratic representation and social justice among working-class people.

The Representation of the People Act 1918 was passed in Britain, granting voting rights to women over the age of 30 and extending suffrage to all men over the age of 21, marking a significant milestone in the expansion of democracy and political participation.

The General Strike took place in Britain, involving millions of workers across various industries protesting against wage cuts and deteriorating working conditions, highlighting tensions between labor and capital and the struggle for workers' rights.

The National Health Service (NHS) was founded in Britain, providing comprehensive healthcare services free at the point of use, reflecting efforts to improve social welfare and address health inequalities through state intervention.

The arrival of the Windrush generation, migrants from the Caribbean who came to Britain to fill labor shortages after World War II, contributed to Britain's cultural diversity and highlighted issues of immigration, citizenship, and racial discrimination.

Devolution referendums were held in Scotland and Wales, leading to the establishment of the Scottish Parliament and the National Assembly for Wales, granting these regions varying degrees of legislative power and self-governance within the United Kingdom.

The United Kingdom held a referendum on its membership in the European Union (EU), resulting in a narrow victory for the "Leave" campaign, leading to ongoing debates, negotiations, and political divisions surrounding Britain's withdrawal from the EU.
