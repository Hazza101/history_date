Edward I was crowned King of England following the death of his father, Henry III, marking the beginning of his reign and a period of significant political, military, and legal reforms in Medieval England.

Edward I launched the First Welsh War, aiming to assert English control over Wales and suppress Welsh resistance, leading to the defeat of the Welsh prince Llywelyn ap Gruffudd and the annexation of territories into the English crown.

The Statute of Westminster 1275 was enacted during Edward I's reign, codifying various legal reforms and administrative measures, including regulations on property rights, criminal law, and the organization of local government.

Edward I conducted the Conquest of Wales, culminating in the defeat of the Welsh forces led by Llywelyn ap Gruffudd and the capture of the rebel leader, resulting in the annexation of Wales into the Kingdom of England.

Edward I convened the Model Parliament in 1295, which included representatives from the clergy, nobility, and commoners, setting a precedent for parliamentary representation and consultation in Medieval England.

Edward I embarked on the Second Scottish War of Independence, aiming to assert English dominance over Scotland and subjugate Scottish resistance under King Robert the Bruce, resulting in a prolonged conflict and Scottish victories at the Battle of Stirling Bridge and the Battle of Bannockburn.

The Statute of Merchants was enacted during Edward I's reign, regulating commercial activities and trade practices in Medieval England, including provisions for debt recovery, currency standards, and the protection of merchants' rights.

Edward I issued the Edict of Expulsion, ordering the expulsion of all Jews from England, seizing their property and assets, and banning their return until the 17th century, reflecting anti-Semitic sentiments and financial motivations.

The Statute of Quo Warranto was passed during Edward I's reign, requiring landholders to prove their legal right to hold their lands and titles, leading to inquiries and legal challenges to feudal privileges and land tenure.

Edward I passed away while on campaign against the Scots, ending his reign as one of the longest-serving and most influential monarchs of Medieval England, leaving behind a legacy of military conquests, legal reforms, and administrative innovations.
